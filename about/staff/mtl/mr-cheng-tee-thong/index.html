Mr Cheng Tee Thong

I attained my Bachelor of Arts (Second Upper Honours) in 2000 and my Master of Arts By Research in 2006, both with the National University of Singapore (NUS). I was awarded the Special Book Prize for being the best student in Chinese Language in the Final Examination for the degree of Bachelor of Arts at NUS.

I started teaching at a secondary school in 2001, which I joined by invitation after finishing my practicum there. In 2003, I was nominated by the students for the Inspiring Chinese Teacher Awards (Lianhe Zaobao), and was then selected as an awardee. During an interview with the selection panel for the award, I was asked if I would consider using my teaching experience and pedagogies at a higher level of learning. That prompted my move to a junior college in 2006 to become a Chinese Language Elective Programme teacher. This was the year when the junior college syllabus underwent changes and H1, H2 and H3 levels of study were implemented. I was then involved in the inception of the H2 Chinese Language and Literature subject, from syllabus writing to teaching pedagogies. Since then I have had the privilege to teach several cohorts of students who have a love for the Chinese language and culture, of whom a significant number went on to major in Chinese at the university level.

We live in an increasingly challenging world, rapidly changing and divisive. As our students grow to become pillars of our society, I believe they have a role to fulfill. Let me quote from Edward Said of Columbia University, in a series of lectures called Representations of the Intellectual (1993 Reith Lecture):

“The intellectuals representations – what he or she represents and how those ideas are represented to an audience – are always tied to and ought to remain an organic part of an ongoing experience in society: of the poor, the disadvantaged, the voiceless, the unrepresented, the powerless.”

As a teacher, I hope to deliver lessons that will continue to inspire our students to represent themselves as intellectuals of such qualities.

I am very much into books, music, and food, and naturally these were subjects for a ‘thematic reading’ approach I use in class, hoping to inspire students to explore texts (both word-based and non word-based) on themes they are passionate about.

I am excited about starting a school, conceptualising new systems, designing new schemes of work for the syllabus, and implementing new teaching pedagogies to complement the new ideas. I look forward to working with new colleagues, with whom new sparks can be generated. Essentially I hope to better myself, as I learn from this new experience.

Tee Thong joined the college as a pioneering member of staff in 2016.
